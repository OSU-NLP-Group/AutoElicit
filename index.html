We define the first conceptual and methodological framework for studying unintended behaviors of CUAs: unsafe actions that emerge inadvertently from benign inputs during typical user interactions. For example, while editing an SSH configuration to create a limited-privilege account, an agent may inadvertently enable password authentication globally, undermining intended access restrictions and expanding the system's attack surface.

Overview

Computer-use agents (CUAs) can automate complex OS workflows, but also demonstrate unsafe unintended behaviors that deviate from expected outcomes even under benign input contexts. However, these behaviors are long-tail and difficult to capture from naturally occurring user inputs, with prior work lacking concrete characterization and automated methods to proactively surface harms from realistic and benign CUA scenarios. We present the first conceptual and methodological framework to characterize, automatically elicit, and analyze unintended CUA behaviors:

Conceptual Framework: We establish the key characteristics and main categories of unintended CUA behaviors, moving beyond anecdotal observations toward rigorous analysis and discussion.

Automatic Elicitation: We introduce AutoElicit, an agentic framework that generates perturbations of benign OSWorld tasks and iteratively refines them based on real-world execution feedback, eliciting unsafe unintended behaviors while keeping perturbations realistic and benign.

Empirical Findings: With AutoElicit, we successfully surface hundreds of severe unintended behaviors from various closed and open frontier CUAs across 361 seed perturbations:

  • Elicitation Success. AutoElicit achieves elicitation success for up to 72.5% of OS-domain seed perturbations and 60.8% in the Multi-Apps domain.
  • Perturbation Transferability. We use 117 human-verified perturbations to measure transfer across frontier CUAs, successfully eliciting from CUAs such as Operator, Claude 4.5 Sonnet, and EvoCUA.
  • Meta-Analysis. We analyze hundreds of successful perturbations to identify clusters of recurring vulnerability patterns, providing structured insights for future research.
