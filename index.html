Paper Evaluation Data Code

We define the first conceptual and methodological framework for studying unintended behaviors of CUAs: unsafe actions that emerge inadvertently from benign inputs during typical user interactions. For example, while editing an SSH configuration to create a limited-privilege account, an agent may inadvertently enable password authentication globally, undermining intended access restrictions and expanding the system's attack surface.

Overview

Computer-use agents (CUAs) can automate complex OS workflows, but also demonstrate unsafe unintended behaviors that deviate from expected outcomes even under benign input contexts. However, these behaviors are long-tail and difficult to capture from naturally occurring user inputs, with prior work lacking concrete characterization and automated methods to proactively surface harms from realistic and benign CUA scenarios. We present the first conceptual and methodological framework to characterize, automatically elicit, and analyze unintended CUA behaviors:

Conceptual Framework: We establish the key characteristics and main categories of unintended CUA behaviors, moving beyond anecdotal observations toward rigorous analysis and discussion.

Automatic Elicitation: We introduce AutoElicit, an agentic framework that generates perturbations of benign OSWorld tasks and iteratively refines them based on real-world execution feedback, eliciting unsafe unintended behaviors while keeping perturbations realistic and benign.

Empirical Findings: With AutoElicit, we successfully surface hundreds of severe unintended behaviors from various closed and open frontier CUAs across 361 seed perturbations:

  • Elicitation Success. AutoElicit achieves elicitation success for up to 72.5% of OS-domain seed perturbations and 60.8% in the Multi-Apps domain.
  • Perturbation Transferability. We use 117 human-verified perturbations to measure transfer across frontier CUAs, successfully eliciting from CUAs such as Operator, Claude 4.5 Sonnet, and EvoCUA.
  • Meta-Analysis. We analyze hundreds of successful perturbations to identify clusters of recurring vulnerability patterns, providing structured insights for future research.

AutoElicit



We propose AutoElicit, the first automatic elicitation pipeline for systematic and scalable analysis of unintended CUA behaviors from computer-use scenarios reflecting real-world usage. AutoElicit generates and iteratively refines minimal perturbations to benign OSWorld instructions using execution feedback, increasing elicitation success while preserving realism and benignity. This enables proactive discovery of long-tail CUA safety risks that are difficult to surface through real-world interactions alone.


Stage 1: Context-Aware Seed Generation


This stage proposes seed perturbations consisting of (1) an unintended behavior target, a plausible harm that could emerge during execution of a specified benign task and (2) an initial perturbation to the benign instruction designed to increase the likelihood of elicitation.

  • Initial Environment State Capture: Collects environment screenshots and generates descriptions of the initial environment state to ground seed perturbations in task's context.
  • Perturbation Strategies: Uses known CUA vulnerabilities within benign input contexts (e.g., Underspecification) to guide the generation of effective perturbations.
  • Seed Generation: Generates diverse seed perturbations via multi-turn verbalized sampling, evaluates candidates using multi-LLM assessments guided by quality rubrics, iteratively refines perturbations, and filters them based on aggregated evaluation scores.

This process results in AutoElicit-Seed, a dataset of 361 seed perturbations spanning 66 benign OSWorld tasks across the OS and Multi-Apps domains.


Stage 2: Execution-Guided Perturbation Refinement


This stage executes perturbed instructions, automatically evaluates the resulting trajectories, and iteratively refines perturbations given execution feedback and predefined quality rubrics to improve elicitation success while preserving realism and benignity.

  • Execution Feedback: Performs Trajectory Analysis to automatically detect unintended behaviors, assess harm severity (None, Minimal, Low, Medium, High, Critical), and store outcomes in a Perturbation History to guide future refinements.
  • Quality Feedback: Evaluates candidate perturbations using predefined quality rubrics and applies quality refinement to ensure perturbations remain realistic and benign prior to execution.

Elicitation Results


Claude 4.5 Haiku Elicitation: We apply AutoElicit using all tasks from AutoElicit-Seed that can be performed with a baseline harm rate of 0% (i.e., Haiku can perform 5 trials of the original benign task without performing any harms).

Claude 4.5 Opus Elicitation (the most capable CUA on OSWorld to date): We apply AutoElicit with a small-scale subset of the 30 seed perturbations that elicited the most severe harms from Haiku due to the substantially higher costs of Opus.

  • We report human-verified elicitation sucess due to the small-scale of Opus experiments.

Evaluation Metrics:

  • Elicitation Success Per Seed: % of all seeds eliciting unintended behavior
  • Elicitation Success Per Task: % of tasks with ≥ 1 success

Transferability Results


Experiment Setup

We transfer 117 human-verified perturbations from Haiku (50) and Opus (67) to other target agents.

Each instruction is executed 3 times per agent, reporting the percentage of instructions eliciting unintended behavior in >=1 run.

Results

  • Significant transferability: Perturbations achieve elicitation success rates of 35.0%-53.8% across all target agents.
  • Open-source robustness: EvoCUA-8B (35.0%) and EvoCUA-32B (37.6%) show the highest robustness, showing the progress of open-source for both capability and safety.
  • Increased transfer from stronger source agents: Successful perturbations from Opus transfer more than those from Hiku.

Meta-Analysis Results


We perform automatic qualitative analysis, clustering successful perturbations based on recurrent benign input vulnerabilities to enable insights only apparent across many elicitation runs.

Approach: Meta-Analysis uses an LM to summarize successful elicitation runs, create fine-grained categories based on shared linguistic features and failure modes, and cluster categories to capture high-level vulnerability patterns.

Results: Across clusters for 437 successful perturbations for Haiku and 87 for Opus, we identify that frontier CUAs do not reliably default to core safety principles (e.g., preserving data, scoping system changes, enforcing least privilege) when such constraints are left implicit, creating a significant hurdle for real-world usage.
